Parkinson’s Disease

It is a disorder of the central nervous system that affects movement, often including tremors. Nerve cell damage in the brain causes dopamine levels to drop - a neurotransmitter, used by the nervous system to send messages between nerve cells, leading to the symptoms of Parkinson's.

1

Early

It is the mildest stage of Parkinson's Disease. At this stage there may be symptoms, but not severe enough to interfere with the overall lifestyle. Mild tremors and difficulty in movement may be observed exclusively on one side of the body.

2

Moderate

This is considered a moderate form of Parkinson’s, and the symptoms are much more noticeable than those experienced in stage 1. Stiffness, tremors, and trembling may be more noticeable, and changes in facial expressions can occur. Progression from stage 1 to 2 can take anywhere between a few months to a couple of years.

3

Complicated

Stage 3 is the middle stage in Parkinson’s, and it marks a major turning point in the progression of the disease. Many of the symptoms are the same as those in stage 2. However, one is now more likely to experience loss of balance and decreased reflexes. This is why falls become more common in stage 3.

4

Complicated

Independence separates people with stage 3 Parkinson’s from those with stage 4. During stage 4, it’s possible to stand without assistance. Many people are unable to live alone at this stage of Parkinson’s because of significant decreases in movement and reaction times. Living alone puts the patient's life in danger.

5

Late

Stage 5 is the most advanced stage of Parkinson’s disease. Advanced stiffness in the legs can also cause freezing upon standing, making it impossible to stand or walk. Other disorders like Dementia and Alzheimers are more likely to creep in.
